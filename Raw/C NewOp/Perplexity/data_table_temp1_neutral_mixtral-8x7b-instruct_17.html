Concatenation (C)
Concatenation is denoted by @ and represents the operation of joining the digits of two numbers together. When using concatenation, one does not consider the signs of the numbers involved, and instead focuses on the absolute values. Here are some examples:

* If a = 12 and b = 56, then a @ b = 12 @ 56 = 1256.
* If a = 12 and b = -56, then a @ b = |a| @ |b| = |12| @ |-56| = 12 @ 56 = 1256.
* If a = 0 and b = 24, then a @ b = 0 @ 24 = 024 = 24.

Prime Factor Counting (F)
Prime Factor Counting is denoted by # and calculates the count of unique prime factors shared between the absolute values of two numbers. Here are some examples:

* If a = 12 and b = 56, then a # b = 12 # 56 = 1, since the prime factors of 12 are 2 and 3, and the prime factors of 56 are 2 and 7, resulting in one shared prime factor, 2.
* If a = -16 and b = 4, then a # b = |a| # |b| = |-16| # |4| = 16 # 4 = 1, since the prime factors of 16 are 2 and the prime factors of 4 are 2 and 5, resulting in one shared prime
